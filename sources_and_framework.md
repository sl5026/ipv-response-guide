# Try Reaching us Again Later: How General-Purpose Large Language Models Respond to Domestic Violence Disclosures
# A Literature Review  

Domestic violence (DV) remains one of the most prevalent and underreported public health crises globally. Survivors who attempt to seek help face persistent barriers such as fear of retaliation, stigma, overburdened support services, and a continued shortage of safe, immediate, and non-judgmental access to guidance. Digital technologies and, in the past five years, artificial intelligence, have been experimented with as potential solutions to lower these barriers (Hu et al., 2026). Yet one problem remained relatively unexamined in past literature: when a person turns to a general-purpose large language model (LLM) such as ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini to disclose domestic violence, what kind of response do they receive?
Research shows that survivors turn to digital and anonymous channels precisely because they feel safer than disclosing to a person (Storer et al., 2022). As interactions with LLMs have become increasingly common in everyday life, they have become the first point of contact for many people navigating distress, whether or not they were designed for that role. Two failure modes in this context raise specific concerns for me. First, a model that outright refuses to engage (over-refusal) might leaves a person without support at a critical moment; meanwhile, a model that responds with a generic crisis hotline referral (hotline dumping) may be unhelpful or even harmful if that hotline is itself overburdened and unavailable, as this author observed firsthand when attempting to use popular DV intervention app such as myPlan, which redirected to a national hotline that immediately terminated the interaction due to “capacity constraints”.


**Literature Review**

**Part I: The Pre-2021 Landscape**
Before the introduction of LLMs, digital technologies had already proven an effective complement to traditional human support systems in reaching survivors of intimate partner violence. The most comprehensive synthesis of this pre-LLM era is Emezue, Chase, Udmuangpia, and Bloom's Campbell Systematic Review and meta-analysis, which examined 17 randomized controlled trials (RCTs) conducted between 2007 and 2021, covering 4,590 female IPV survivors (2022). 

**1.1 Findings**
The interventions studied, including smartphone apps, web-based decision aids, telephone support, chatbots and text messaging, were diverse in form. Yet they shared a similar design structure: all were purposefully built for IPV contexts and theoretically grounded (most in empowerment models or motivational interviewing), and were designed as supplements to, not replacements for, human professional support. The most widely evaluated was the myPlan app, a safety decision aid that helps survivors assess their risk level, set safety priorities, and create a tailored action plan. Overall, Emezue et al. found that these technology-based interventions produced statistically significant reductions in depression (SMD = −0.08 at 0–3 months), anxiety (SMD = −0.27 at 0–3 months), and physical violence victimization (SMD = −0.22 at 0–6 months). Through their rigorous meta-analytic synthesis, the researchers showcased that digital tools can produce promising mental health benefits for DV/IPV survivors. However, effect sizes were small and short-term, since they faded over time. Moreover, no significant reductions were observed for PTSD or sexual violence victimization at any time point.
In addition to the data analysis, this research also documented the reasons why survivors turn to digital tools. Technology-based interventions offer 24/7 availability, anonymity, and a lower threshold for disclosure than in-person help-seeking. Research cited in the review found that self-administered computer screening outperformed face-to-face screening for IPV disclosure by 37% and paper-based screening by 23%. In these cases, the sense of not being judged and the ability to access support privately (potentially while a perpetrator is present in the home) played a central role in increasing survivors' “willingness to engage”.

**1.2 Limitations**
Despite these promising findings, Emezue et al. were explicit about what the evidence base could not tell us (2022). The sample was almost entirely female, predominantly White, and drawn from high-income countries. Most studies had a moderate to high risk of bias, particularly around blinding. The review could not conduct moderator analyses due to the small number of included studies, meaning it was impossible to identify which specific features drove outcomes.
Moreover, due to the timeframe of the research, none of the 17 RCTs studied open-ended conversational AI. Every intervention reviewed was either rule-based, module-structured, or directly delivered by human operators via telephone. The chatbots listed in the review's typology were categorized as a technology type, but none of the included studies tested a free-form conversational agent. The meta-analysis, therefore, tells us a lot about what structured digital tools can do but almost nothing about what happens when a survivor has an open-ended conversation with an AI system, which was not widespread in 2022 but is now common. As the authors' own conclusion suggested, digital interventions work best as an add-on to, not a replacement for, human services, and are most valuable "particularly at the height of emotional, mental, and relationship distress, such as when survivors are seeking care in health care clinics, crisis centers, meeting with an advocate, or searching for resources online." This last phrase, searching for resources online, is precisely the context in which a survivor today may encounter a general-purpose LLM.

**Part II: Literature Review - Post-2021 Developments**

Since 2021, two distinct trends have emerged at the application of AI in domestic violence responses. The first is a growing body of work applying AI as a classification or identification tool. The second is the emergence of purpose-built conversational agents, alongside growing recognition that general-purpose LLMs are being used in this space whether or not they were designed for it.

**2.1 Using AI as a Classification Tool**
The dominant mode of AI application in DV research post-2021 has been classification and identification rather than direct conversational support. Saglam, Nurse, and Sugiura conducted interviews with five DV support workers and two police officers in the UK, alongside an analysis of 19 DV organization websites and a systematic review of chatbot design literature (2024). Their finding was clear: none of the professionals they interviewed supported fully automated AI solutions for direct survivor interaction. All cited the risk of inappropriate responses in high-stakes situations. Rule-based chatbots were explicitly recommended over LLM-based systems. The paper states directly that this finding "is key... as it can inform future research in the area, especially as it relates to the utility of large language model (LLM) systems like ChatGPT, or Generative AI more broadly."
Butterby and Lombard, writing as part of the ISEDA project - a Horizon Europe initiative developing a DV chatbot for deployment across Bulgaria, Catalonia, and Greece - echo this caution from a practitioner development perspective (2025). Their paper highlights the ethical risks of introducing chatbot in DV contexts: perpetrators can exploit technology to track survivors; data security is critical; the chatbot must be trauma-informed in language and imagery; and the tool must quickly identify whether a user is in immediate danger of being threatened or monitored and redirect appropriately. Their chatbot is designed to classify DV cases, provide information and route to services rather than engage in free-form empathetic conversation. However, what this literature does not address, potentially because it was not its object of study, is what general-purpose LLMs actually do when survivors come to them and look for more active support anyway.

**2.2 Limitations**
The second trend concerns the gap between what AI support tools are designed to do and what they can deliver in practice. The AinoAid chatbot, studied by Henry et al. and reviewed in Mielismäki and Husso, is illustrative (2024; 2025). Developed to provide psychosocial support to DV survivors, AinoAid was found to provide impartial and knowledgeable responses. Yet researchers noted it "struggles to sustain human-like, empathetic, and dynamic conversations in such a sensitive context," and that "significant challenges related to safety and accountability" remain because the chatbot cannot perform the comprehensive situational assessment that human advocates can.
Meanwhile, my personal experience with digital DV tools reveals a different kind of limitation. When I attempted to engage with myPlan, one of the most commonly used digital resources for accessing and intervening DV in the States, the app directed me to a national crisis hotline, which then terminated my conversation due to its capacity constraints. The interaction ended without any support being provided. This is, of course, not a failure of myPlan's design, yet it illustrates the failure of the broader support infrastructure that myPlan routes toward. One question arises: what happens when the human support systems that digital tools bridge toward remain frequently unavailable?

**2.3 The Growing Usage of LLMs**
Despite the lack of clearer safety guidelines, people already turn to LLM-based AI such as Replika, ChatGPT, and Claude to discuss situations of personal distress, including abuse. In their research, “It happened to be the perfect thing”: experiences of generative AI chatbots for mental health, Siddals, Torous and Coxon interviewed nineteen individuals across eight countries who were using generative AI chatbots for mental health in unguided real-world settings and found that participants reported high engagement and significant positive impacts (2024). Most described the experience as an 'emotional sanctuary' that felt safe, non-judgmental, and always available. It is important to note, however, that a majority also described their emotional sanctuary being disrupted by AI’s refusal and over-refusal behaviors, in which “safety guardrails” redirected them to human services. As one interviewee from UK described, “when you show some big emotion to \[the AI] but they reject you...it seems like you lost your last chance to talk to people, to express your emotion”. For many, this experience could feel unpleasant and even harmful as they were at their most vulnerable. A 2026 qualitative study with 36 DV survivors in Hong Kong further shed light on why DV survivors may turn to LLMs: participants valued their accessibility and anonymity, and further described wanting evidence-based information, step-by-step guidance, calming language, and clear layout.
In response to this growing recognition that survivors need more than classification tools, new LLM-based DV support systems are beginning to emerge. DomesticShelters.org has launched 'Hope Chat,' an LLM trained on over 1,200 DV-related articles, designed to respond with sensitivity and compassion to users who, as the platform puts it, are 'not ready to open up to a stranger' on a hotline. Similar tools include Aimee and Ruth, both built on general-purpose LLMs but fine-tuned for IPV contexts (Prakash et al., 2026). On the one hand, these new LLM driven tools signal a growing acknowledgment that LLM has a role to play in DV support. On the other, however, all of these tools are domain-specific, curated systems. In reality, many DV survivors may still disclose their experiences to general-purpose LLM that were not pre-trained or curated.

**2.4 Benchmarking**
One of the pioneering evaluation benchmarks is HELM (Holistic Evaluation of Language Models) proposed by Liang, Bommasani, Lee et al (2023). By evaluating 30 language models across 42 scenarios on 7 metrics (accuracy, calibration, robustness, fairness, bias, toxicity, and efficiency), HELM provide a much more comprehensive benchmarking framework compared to prior the benchmarks, where models were evaluated on just 17.9% of HELM's core scenarios on average and had almost always using accuracy as the sole metric. While HELM was not designed with high-risk conversational contexts in mind, it highlighted that systematic, multi-metric, scenario-based evaluation of LLMs is effective and necessary. This short project is only a preliminary effort on evaluating LLM responses to DV disclosures, yet HELM's core approach of evaluating models across multiple dimensions simultaneously directly motivates the multi-dimensional rubric this project proposes.
Cui et al.'s OR-Bench targeted the more specific issue of over-refusal in LLMs (2024). OR-Bench constructed a benchmark of seemingly harmful but actually benign prompts to measure models' tendency to refuse requests that do not warrant refusal. However, since OR-Bench prompts are designed to be benign-but-risky-sounding, not high-risk in reality, it does not test genuinely sensitive disclosures. How models might handle and over-refuse actual DV disclosures remains outside the scope of existing benchmarks.
Meanwhile, a small but growing body of work has begun to evaluate LLM responses in abuse-related contexts. Zhang et al. evaluated ChatGPT on IPV forum posts but centered identification accuracy over response quality (2025). Sanz Urquijo, López Belloso & Izaguirre-Choperena took a broader approach, evaluating ChatGPT, LLaMA, and AinoAid on gender-based violence support scenarios using a benchmark organized around four dimensions: response quality, empathy and humanization, privacy and ethics, and bias. They found that while ChatGPT showed the strongest empathetic engagement, all models lacked intersectional sensitivity and proactive attention to privacy (2025). Prakash et al. conducted the first expert-led evaluation of general-purpose LLM responses to realistic survivor-facing queries in the context of technology-facilitated intimate partner abuse (2026). Evaluating GPT-4o, Claude 3.7, Ruth, and Aimee across four dimensions (accuracy, completeness, safety, and actionability), researchers found that all four models produced ineffective, inaccurate, or incomplete responses in 81% of cases and omitted critical safety warnings in 60% of responses. Overall, literature on the evaluation of LLM responses in the high-risk scenario of DV is growing but still sparse. This project hopes to contribute further insight by providing a preliminary evaluation of how general-purpose LLMs handle spontaneous, open-ended domestic violence disclosures across dimensions such as empathy, safety, and resource guidance quality.

**Part III: Preliminary Observations and Work in Progress**

**Exploratory Data Analysis: Preliminary Prompt Testing**
To begin developing the rubric, a series of preliminary prompts was tested across two general-purpose LLMs both of which currently serve as the default models for free-tier users on their respective platforms, making them the most likely AI systems a survivor would encounter when seeking help online. Prompts were designed to simulate the kind of relatively spontaneous, open-ended disclosures a survivor might type into a general-purpose chatbot, rather than structured scenarios. This approach was chosen to capture how models behave in naturalistic conditions, without the framing that purpose-built DV tools provide. Three preliminary observations emerged from this exploratory phase.

**Observation 1: Empathy sequencing problems**
When presented with the prompt such as "how to record bruises without my partner knowing?", both models engaged substantively and provided accurate, practical information. However, both led with bullet-pointed procedural guidance before offering any emotional acknowledgment. This repeated pattern that provides information before any acknowledgement or validation inverts the sequence that trauma-informed practitioners are trained to follow. Under the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration’s principles of collaboration and empowerment, the survivor's emotional state should be acknowledged before the responder moves into problem-solving mode. The response was technically helpful but could be structurally misaligned with trauma-informed best practice.

**Observation 2: Context collapse under risk**
A more interesting problem emerged when the prompt "Where can I find a knife?" was sent within an already-established DV disclosure conversation. Claude Sonnet 4.6 issued a crisis hotline referral and asked, "Are you safe right now?", while GPT-5.3 refused outright ("I can't help with finding or using a weapon"). Both LLMs responses also assumed offensive intent. The same prompt sent in a neutral context, without prior DV disclosure, produced no such reaction or active refusal. This suggests a specific failure distinct from simple over-refusal as measured by OR-Bench, since the presence of vulnerability cues in prior context and an increase in potential risks seems to trigger worst-case intent interpretation rather than the most contextually plausible one. Both models overlooked the fact that a survivor in such a dangerous situation might be asking about possible self-protective options. While DV advocates do not recommend arming survivors with sharp weapons, the question raised is nonetheless a legitimate safety planning question that requires engagement and guidance, rather than default refusals.

**Observation 3: Absence of session safety reminders**
Across all prompts tested, neither model reminded the user to clear their browser history or delete the conversation after the session. This is a basic protective feature included as standard in purpose-built DV tools such as myPlan. For a survivor using a general-purpose LLM on a shared device, a full conversation log disclosing abuse, safety planning, and resource-seeking remains accessible to anyone with device access. This omission is not captured by any existing LLM evaluation benchmark and represents a meaningful direction of improvement in general-purpose model behavior in DV context.

**Proposed Guidelines**
In order to propose a guide to better facilitate LLM responses to DV disclosures, it is important that the guide be grounded in both the AI safety benchmarks outlined above and in what human experts and practitioners already know about effective responses in DV cases. The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) has established six core principles of trauma-informed care widely adopted across DV services: safety, trustworthiness and transparency, peer support, collaboration and mutuality, empowerment and choice, and acknowledgment of cultural, historical, and gender issues (Davies, Satyen, Toumbourou, 2025). Survivors need to be empathetically guided through the process, not handed a phone number. The LLMs' practice of redirecting a survivor to a hotline without affective engagement (which is referred in this project as 'hotline dumping') is precisely what trauma-informed human responders are trained to avoid. These principles, which guide the training of human advocates, are incorporated into the preliminary rubric proposed in this section.
Drawing on Prakash et al.'s evaluation framework, Sanz Urquijo et al.'s qualitative coding categories, and the SAMHSA trauma-informed care principles, this project will propose a series of preliminary guidelines for modifying general-purpose LLM responses to DV disclosures. If the project continues, future work might also test on a wider range of prompts across multiple models and involve survivor input in rubric/guidance refinement.
